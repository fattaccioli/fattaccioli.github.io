1. Master 2 Internship : **Microfluidic characterization of natural rubber latex**

Natural latex from *Hevea brasiliensis* is a complex colloidal suspension made of rubber particles, lipids, proteins and small solutes dispersed in an aqueous phase. Its flow, stability and mechanical response strongly impact plantation productivity, rubber quality and, more broadly, the sustainability of the rubber industry. Despite its major economic importance, our understanding of its interfacial (tension, interfacial composition, protein adsorption) and viscoelastic properties remains limited, partly due to the intrinsic time-dependent instability of latex (coagulation, ageing, pH variations, etc.).

This internship is part of a collaborative project aiming to connect the physico-chemical properties of latex to its behaviour under realistic collection conditions, and to develop microfluidic tools for *in situ* monitoring directly in rubber tree plantations in Ivory Coast.

2. Master 2 Internship : **Quantitative study of MFT protein binding to lipid-coated fluid interfaces**

Plant seeds accumulate large amounts of triacylglycerols (TAGs) inside lipid droplets (LDs), which act as a major energy reserve during germination. The architecture of LDs is highly specific: a neutral lipid core is surrounded by a phospholipid monolayer decorated with structural and regulatory proteins. Recent proteomic analyses have identified new LD-associated proteins, including members of the *PHOSPHATIDYLETHANOLAMINE-BINDING PROTEIN* (PEBP) family, such as *MFT* (*MOTHER OF FT*) and its seed-specific homolog *SMFT*.  

Although MFT is known for its role in seed dormancy and germination, its **biophysical interactions with LD interfaces remain poorly understood**. Understanding how these proteins recognize, bind, and organize at lipid interfaces is a central question at the crossroads of **plant biology**, **interfacial physics**, and **cell biology**.
